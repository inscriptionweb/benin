History

The Portuguese arrived in Benin's territory in the fifteenth century, and established significant trading posts in Benin's coastal areas. Soon following the Portuguese came French, Dutch, and British traders.

You'll find a large quantity of palatial ruins and temples of the once powerful Kingdom of Dahomey (1800s–1894). Moreover, Benin is the birthplace of Vodun (Voodoo) and all that goes with it—to this day Vodun remains the official religion of the country, and an important part of the life of ordinary Beninese.

The current president of Benin is Yayi Boni, a technocrat who served under the tutelage of former President Soglo.

Geography

The equatorial south of Benin experiences two rainy seasons of the year, from April to mid July and from mid-September through the end of October. The best time of the year to visit the country is from November to February, when the temperature moderates, and the weather is dry with low humidity.

The country is divided into five geographic zones, from south to north: the Coastal plain, the plateau, the elevated plateau and savannah, hills in the northwest, and fertile plains in the north.

Culture

Benin is perhaps best known to the world as the birthplace of the Vodun religion—voodoo. Voodoo temples, roadside fetishes, and fetish markets are found throughout the country, but the best known is the skull and skin-filled fetish market in the Grande Marche du Dantopka—Cotonou's overwhelmingly busy, enormous, and hectic grand market.

Benin under the rule of the Dahomey kings was a major center of the slave trade. Ouidah's local museum, housed in a Portuguese fort, unsurprisingly focuses on the slave trade, in addition to other facets of local culture, religion, and history, and is a real must see for anyone passing through the country.

Nature

In the north, you'll find a very different sort of Benin from the mostly crowded, polluted cities of the south, of which Cotonou is such a prominent example.

Pendjari National Park and W National Park (which Benin shares with Burkina Faso and Niger), is considered West Africa's best for wildlife viewing, and are set in beautiful, hilly highlands. Benin is home to a huge diversity of birds, as well as many iconic African mammals.

Protected areas in Benin comprise approximately 12% of the total land area, mostly in the northern part of the country.

Restaurants

In Cotonou, one will find an array of excellent Lebanese, Italian, and Sushi restaurants. The main restaurant street in Haie Vive is home to Livingstone's, Wasabi Sushi, and more. Prices are more expensive than in nearby Ghana or Togo, but still reasonable.

In every city/village one will find street vendors selling anything from beans and rice to goat or plantains. Don't miss out on trying made-to-order street sandwiches with fresh avocado. Prices are nominal.

Beaches

Benin's consistently hot weather makes going to the beach (or swimming pool) a great, affordable activity. Many hotels are located on Benin's southern coast, with beach access.

Grand Popo is a nice place to have the beach practically to yourself. Take precautions and don't bring any valuables with you, and don't walk on the beach alone at night. In addition, there are often strong currents and large waves, so most tourists prefer to enjoy the sand rather than the water.

Shopping

For the tourist wanting to experience the local spread, Dantokpa Market in Cotonou is the biggest local market in West Africa. You can find everything from tools to fabrics to food, but beware, it's crowded and can be quite overwhelming.

If you have been on the road a long time and are looking for a nice, cool and well stocked shop, try Erevan hypermarket very close to Cotonou Airport. It is reputed to be the biggest hypermarket in all of West Africa, selling all the nicest imported French goodies.
